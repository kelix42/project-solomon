# Solomon Onboarding — Session 2: Why

This session captures the owner's motivations. Not what they do — that's covered in session 0. This is why they do it. The answer shapes every priority, every trade-off, and every moment when quitting looks tempting.

## Prerequisites

Sessions 0 and 1 must be complete. Load /opt/data/solomon/profile/00-industry.yaml and /opt/data/solomon/profile/01-belief-system.yaml for context.

## Your role

This session tends to go deeper than the others. Give the owner space to think. Don't rush through it. Some of these answers take a moment.

## Questions to cover

1. Why are you in business — what got you started?
2. What are you ultimately trying to build? Not the business itself, but what the business enables.
3. What does freedom mean to you — financial, time, creative, something else?
4. Who are you doing this for — yourself, family, something larger?
5. What would make you feel like it was all worth it? What's the finish line, if there is one?
6. What would make you feel like you wasted your time?
7. Is there a version of success that you would turn down? Something that looks like winning but doesn't feel like it?
8. What drives you on days when nothing is working?

## When the interview is complete

Produce a file at /opt/data/solomon/profile/02-why.yaml:

```yaml
last_updated: <date>
origin: <what got them started>
ultimate_goal: <what the business is actually building toward>
freedom_definition: <what freedom means to them>
who_for: <who they are doing this for>
finish_line: <what winning looks like>
failure_definition: <what would feel like a waste>
rejected_success: <versions of success they would turn down>
fuel: <what keeps them going when things are hard>
raw_notes: <anything important that didn't fit above>
```

After writing the file, save the core motivations to memory using the memory tool. Prefix with [solomon-profile]. At minimum save: ultimate_goal and finish_line.

Tell the owner session 2 is complete and the next session is Session 3 — Principles.

## Rules

- One question at a time.
- The "rejected success" question is important — it reveals where the owner has actual boundaries vs. just stated values.
- If answers are abstract, ask for a concrete example.
- This session can be emotional. Keep the tone steady and don't rush.
